# Art Direction

Status: visual identity direction for future OceanGame art work. This is a planning document, not implemented production art.

## Style Summary

OceanGame uses a side-view 2D alien-ocean expedition diorama style. The player pilots a tiny warm-lit research sub from a cozy surface lab into layered underwater regions that become darker, stranger, and more dangerous with depth. The style should combine readable silhouettes, parallax depth, soft underwater lighting, glowing alien biology, pressure-locked wrecks/ruins, and clean sci-fi/cozy UI. Visuals must support route choice, oxygen pressure, cargo decisions, scanning, predator avoidance, and the desire to go deeper.

## Visual Pillars

- Tiny research sub against a vast alien ocean.
- Warm safe surface base versus strange dangerous deep.
- Persistent geography with variable daily opportunity.
- Readable routes before decorative detail.
- Scan targets and resources must be visually identifiable.
- Predator danger must be readable before contact.
- Deep rewards should be visible enough to tempt the player before commitment.
- UI should be clean, readable, and charming, not debug-first.

## Anti-Dave Rules

Dave the Diver is useful only as a principle reference for side-view underwater readability and polish level. OceanGame should not visually center on:

- tropical scuba diving,
- human diver silhouette,
- fishing or spearing action,
- restaurant/comedy UI,
- normal fish as the main reward fantasy,
- bright tropical coral everywhere.

Instead, OceanGame should center on:

- research sub,
- scanning,
- field analysis,
- alien organisms,
- pressure boundaries,
- mysterious wrecks/ruins,
- daily ocean anomalies,
- warm safe base versus dark strange deep.

## Palette Direction

- Surface Base: warm amber, soft white, safety cyan.
- Shallows: cyan, teal, sunlit blue, gentle pink/purple coral.
- Midwater: darker teal, lavender kelp, slate rock, distant wreck silhouettes.
- Wreck Shelf: rust orange, slate blue, pale work lights, broken metal silhouettes.
- Thermal Vents: sulfur gold, black teal, magenta biological glow.
- Pressure Trench: indigo black, sparse green/blue bioluminescence, red danger accents.
- Alien Ruins: violet, cold white, impossible red, geometric shapes.

Keep color role-based. Do not make every important object bright, and do not make every region a reef.

## Shape Language

- Surface/base: clean human-made shapes, circles, rectangles, warm windows.
- Shallows: rounded coral, open water, gentle plant movement.
- Midwater: taller kelp forms, larger rock shelves, broken silhouettes.
- Wreck shelf: angular metal, pipes, cracked panels.
- Thermal vents: vertical chimneys, mineral stacks, heat shimmer.
- Pressure trench: huge dark walls, narrow negative space, giant silhouettes.
- Alien ruins: unnatural geometry, symmetry, pressure doors, glyph-like scan markings.

## Player Sub Design Notes

The sub is the game's visual anchor:

- small rounded research sub,
- large front viewport,
- warm yellow/orange hull or warm accent lights,
- soft nose light cone,
- visible bubble trail,
- small scanner antenna/dish,
- readable silhouette facing left/right,
- fragile, curious, scientific, not military.

The sub should feel brave and vulnerable in a huge ocean. It should not read like a weapon platform.

## Resource Visual Rules

Resources should have distinct readable silhouettes:

- Kelp Fiber: shallow, soft waving plant/strand.
- Shell Fragments: midwater, pale shard cluster.
- Glow Plankton: deep, bioluminescent cluster.
- Future resources should be distinct by silhouette, color, and region.

Resources should read as cargo before decoration. Pickup art may become prettier, but its gameplay identity must stay legible at normal camera scale.

## Scan Effect Visual Language

Scanning should become a signature visual action:

- soft cone or pulse from the sub,
- target outline or reticle,
- small data glyph/field-note feel,
- nearby resource pulse when scan has a tactical effect,
- avoid cluttering the screen with too much text.

The scanner should feel like field analysis, not a quest marker system. Use partial information, pulses, and target outlines rather than long world labels.

## Predator Visual Rules

Predators should have readable states:

- patrol/safe distance,
- warning/near danger,
- detection/chase,
- contact/hit feedback,
- return-to-patrol.

The player must understand danger before contact. Predator visuals should communicate route timing and risk, not combat loot.

## UI Direction

Separate player-facing UI from debug/playtest UI.

Player-facing UI:

- oxygen gauge,
- depth,
- base direction,
- compact cargo slots with filled, empty, and locked states,
- current scan target,
- low/critical oxygen warning with compact panel placement that stays out of the active route/control text stack.

Debug/playtest UI:

- seed,
- pattern,
- telemetry,
- oxygen at result,
- failure cause.

Debug UI should stay behind a debug toggle before any public demo. Player-facing UI should feel clean, readable, and lightly cozy-science-fiction rather than raw prototype telemetry.

## Target Feeling

OceanGame should feel like a cozy research expedition into a beautiful but increasingly impossible alien ocean. The surface should feel human and safe; the deeper ocean should feel beautiful, strange, and eventually hostile. The player should want to dive again because a visible route, creature behavior, scan echo, pressure door, or glow in the dark suggests that tomorrow they can understand or reach a little more.
